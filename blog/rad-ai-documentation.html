Behind the Paper: Documenting AI Systems for the EU AI Act

2026-03-30 3 min read
TL;DR

Standard software architecture documentation (arc42 + C4) covers only about 36% of what the EU AI Act demands. RAD-AI adds 8 new arc42 sections and 3 new C4 diagram types, reaching approximately 93% coverage of Annex IV. Compliance becomes an engineering artifact, not a legal afterthought.

The Problem

The EU AI Act requires detailed technical documentation for high-risk AI systems, codified in Annex IV. The intent is reasonable: if you deploy an AI system that affects people's lives, you should be able to explain how it works, what data it uses, how it was tested, and how it is monitored. The problem is that existing software architecture documentation standards were not designed with AI in mind.

We audited two of the most widely adopted documentation frameworks, arc42 and C4, against every requirement in Annex IV. The result was sobering: combined, they cover roughly 36% of the mandatory documentation items. Entire categories like data governance, bias assessment, human oversight mechanisms, and post-deployment monitoring have no natural home in either framework. Teams end up producing compliance documentation in parallel with their engineering documentation, creating redundancy, drift, and a legal-technical gap that nobody owns.

Our Approach

RAD-AI (Reference Architecture Documentation for AI) extends arc42 rather than replacing it. We added 8 new sections that map directly to Annex IV gaps: training data provenance, fairness and bias evaluation, human oversight design, risk classification rationale, post-market monitoring, logging and auditability, accuracy and robustness metrics, and intended-use boundary specification.

On the diagramming side, we introduced 3 new C4 diagram types that capture AI-specific structure. A Data Lineage Diagram traces the full pipeline from raw data collection through preprocessing, training, and inference. A Model Lifecycle Diagram shows versioning, retraining triggers, and rollback paths. A Monitoring Topology Diagram maps where observability hooks exist and what they measure.

Results

With RAD-AI applied, documentation coverage of Annex IV rises to approximately 93%. We validated this by retroactively applying the framework to two well-documented ML platforms: Uber's Michelangelo and Netflix's Metaflow. In both cases, the RAD-AI template surfaced documentation gaps that the original engineering documentation did not address, particularly around data governance, bias testing, and human-in-the-loop escalation paths.

The remaining 7% consists of items that are inherently organization-specific, such as naming the designated legal representative or specifying the exact CE-marking process, which no generic template can or should prescribe.

Implications

The deeper point is about workflow, not compliance. When documentation is an afterthought, it decays. When it is embedded in the engineering process, it becomes a design tool. RAD-AI makes it possible for an ML team to produce Annex IV-compliant documentation as a side effect of doing their normal architecture work. The legal obligation becomes an engineering artifact that stays current because it lives where the engineers already work.

For organizations operating under the AI Act, this means compliance is no longer a separate workstream owned by legal. It is an extension of the architecture documentation that engineering already maintains.
